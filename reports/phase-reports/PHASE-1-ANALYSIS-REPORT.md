## 2. Root cause of the page inflation (confirmed, not guessed)

Found in the shared CSS:

```css
.page{
  width:var(--page-w);       /* 210mm */
  min-height:var(--page-h);  /* 297mm  <-- this is the bug */
  ...
  page-break-after:always;
  overflow:hidden;           /* <-- has no effect, see below */
}
```

`min-height` (not `height`) means a `.page` div is **free to grow taller than one A4 page** if its content needs more room. Because it's `min-height`, `overflow:hidden` never actually clips anything — there's nothing to clip, the box just expands. When Chromium paginates for print, any `.page` div taller than 297mm gets an automatic hard break inserted partway through it — and then the div's own `page-break-after:always` fires at its true end. One authored "page" becomes two, three, sometimes four physical printed pages.

Content budget per page: `297mm − 22mm (top) − 26mm (bottom) = 249mm` usable height, at 166mm content width (210 − 28 − 16 margins).

**This is the critical finding, and it changes the strategy:**

Overflow magnitude ranges from **17.7mm to 281.5mm over budget**, median **~120mm** — nearly half a page, on the *typical* overflowing page. The worst case (Chapter 3, page 12) overflows by 281.5mm — almost an entire extra A4 page's worth of content crammed into one `.page` div, driven by a `.photo-plate` + `.figure.compact` stack that alone total over 200mm.

This is not primarily a CSS/spacing bug that whitespace-trimming can fix. It's a **content-to-page assignment problem**: many pages simply have more figures/photo-plates/tables/activities stacked on them than 249mm of vertical space can hold — likely because pages were composed without measuring real rendered component heights (a 16:9 photo-plate at full 166mm content width alone renders at ~93mm; stack two of those plus a caption and a paragraph and you're already over budget).

## 5. Why this matters for your target (255–258 pages)

Your spec's Module 4 priority list leads with whitespace/spacing reduction (priorities 1–4) before resorting to moving content or adding pages (priorities 5–8). Given the measured data:

- **Whitespace/spacing trimming alone cannot close a 120–280mm gap** without visibly shrinking real photos, figures, and recap boxes well beyond what "preserve appearance exactly" allows. Trimming margins and gaps can realistically reclaim maybe 10–20mm per page — nowhere near enough for the median (120mm) or worst-case (281mm) overflows.
- For the majority of the 148 overflowing pages, the only fix that **preserves appearance exactly** (your hard constraint) is priority 8: **split the overflow content onto a new continuation page**, i.e., we insert additional `.page` divs where content genuinely doesn't fit.
- That means the honest, achievable outcome is **not** 255–258 pages. It's closer to **the true content-driven page count** — likely somewhere in the 300s once we correctly repaginate so nothing is clipped or distorted, *not* the 402 you get today, but well above 258.

I'd rather tell you this now, before building the pipeline, than build 10 modules chasing a target the content itself can't hit without shrinking real images and boxes past a reasonable point.

## 6. Recommended path forward — needs your decision

Three honest options, not mutually exclusive per-chapter:

1. **Repaginate correctly (recommended).** Keep every component's visual size and appearance untouched. Move overflow content onto genuinely new pages so nothing is clipped, distorted, or squeezed. Final count will land wherever the content actually requires (rough estimate based on today's overflow volume: **~300–330 pages** for the book) — this becomes the new honest "HTML page count," and PDF will match it almost exactly (255–258-style precision, just against the *correct* base number, not 254).
2. **Aggressive shrink-to-fit.** Apply meaningful (not 5%-per-iteration-timid) size reductions to photo-plates, figures, and recap boxes to hit closer to 254–258. This *will* change appearance — smaller photos/figures than currently authored — which conflicts with your "preserve appearance exactly" instruction, so I don't recommend it unless you explicitly accept that trade-off for specific component types (e.g., "photo-plates can shrink up to 20%" as an exception).
3. **Hybrid.** Modest, safe spacing/whitespace optimization everywhere (no visible change) *plus* repagination for the pages that still don't fit after that — this is what I'd build into Module 4 regardless of which target we pick, since it reduces how many new pages get inserted in option 1.

My recommendation: **go with option 1, with option 3's spacing optimization included as a first-pass reducer.** It's the only option that satisfies "preserve appearance exactly" as a hard rule while actually fixing the print behavior, and it still produces a tight, predictable, professional page count — just not artificially forced down to 254–258.
